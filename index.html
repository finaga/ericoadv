01 — Context

What is arbitration,
and why does it matter?

Arbitration is private court. Instead of a public judge, both companies agree in their contracts to resolve disputes before a private expert they choose. The decision is binding — just like a judge's ruling.

In Brazil, arbitration has grown steadily for two decades. It dominates infrastructure, oil & gas, construction, and corporate disputes. B3-listed companies are legally required to use it. Multinational corporations default to it for cross-border deals. And tech companies — Uber, iFood, and their peers — use it specifically to keep disputes out of the news.

"Erico handles both private arbitration and Superior Court appeals when arbitration awards are challenged. Most arbitration specialists in São Paulo can't say that. His Brasília base is a structural advantage, not an accident of geography."

Confidentiality

The dispute never becomes public record. Critical for tech companies protecting trade secrets, platform mechanics, or investor relationships.

Speed

Months, not years. Brazilian court backlogs are well-documented. Arbitration sidesteps the queue entirely.

Expertise

The arbitrator is a specialist chosen by the parties — not a generalist judge who may never have seen a platform commerce dispute.

International enforceability

Awards are enforceable in 150+ countries via the New York Convention. Essential for any cross-border business relationship.

Finality

No appeals circus. The decision is final. Companies can move on instead of spending years in procedural limbo.

02 — Diagnosis

Erico's current site —
an honest assessment

Working What to keep

  • ·
    erico.law — premium domain. Short, bilingual, memorable. Rare for a Brazilian firm.
  • ·
    The tagline — "Tribunais Superiores. Disputas Complexas. Arbitragem." Three punches. No padding. Genuine specialist signal. Keep the concept.
  • ·
    Bilingual intent — correct for the international arbitration market. Arbitration clients are frequently multinational.
  • ·
    Focused specialization — correctly avoids generalism. Narrow is right for this market and this client profile.

Broken What needs fixing

  • ·
    Visual identity — no distinctive style. Nothing that signals "Brasília," "premium," or "this specific person." Could be any lawyer anywhere.

    Erico is invisible — solo practice's biggest asset is the individual. The site hides him. No personality, no point of view.

    No client proof — no mention of notable clients, sectors, or types of disputes handled. Even anonymized references build trust.

    Arbitration unexplained — the site assumes visitors already understand arbitration. Most business clients don't. Silence leaves money on the table.
  • ·
    No design system — no visual language that extends to documents, proposals, LinkedIn. Brand must travel beyond the website.

05 — Benchmarks

What premium
boutique looks like

The best-performing boutique law firm sites in 2025–2026 achieve one thing: they look like a SaaS product but feel like a 100-year institution.

Elements that work

Hero Full-bleed image (city, architecture, or abstract geometry) + one short, confident statement
Typography Architectural serif or condensed sans-serif, large, unapologetic. Not body copy scaled up — genuine display type.
Color Deep neutrals (black, charcoal) + one warm accent. White space as a luxury signal, not filler.
Imagery Bold architectural photography or minimal abstract geometry. Never stock photos of scales or courthouses.
Content Specific case references or client types — not generic promises. "We represented a national platform in a BRL 200M dispute" beats "experienced in complex disputes."
Attorney One photograph, done right. Real, not posed-in-a-library. The person IS the brand in a solo practice.

Solo firm design leaders

AC Rieman Centers the attorney's photograph with minimal navigation. The person is the brand. Clean authority without clutter.
Page Law (CA) Elegant, lawyer-centered, accessible without sacrificing authority. Information hierarchy respects the reader's time.
Luqman Law Cityscape hero image, bold typography, simple navigation. City as visual identity done well.
Hallisky Law Mid-page navigation with appointment booking. Functional efficiency as brand signal — no visual excess.
SaaS Law Tech-company aesthetic for a legal practice serving tech clients. Brand mirrors the client's world — strategic empathy.

Common mistakes all competitors are making

  • Listing 5–11 generic values (trust, excellence, commitment, professionalism, resolution)
  • Text-heavy service descriptions that assume the reader already wants to hire you
  • Stock photos of scales of justice, courthouse steps, or men in suits shaking hands
  • Dark navy sidebar with gold text — overused across Brazilian legal market
  • CV-style about pages: graduated from X, member of Y, admitted to Z bar
  • Burying contact information below the fold or behind a form wall
  • No mobile experience — arbitration clients are traveling executives reading on phones

06 — Identity

The Brasília
visual opportunity

Erico loves his city. His city is one of the most visually distinctive capitals on earth. And almost no Brazilian law firm uses it — not even the ones based there.

Oscar Niemeyer's design language — bold geometric forms, large white surfaces against open sky, the tension between curves and straight lines, dramatic negative space, pilotis creating breathing room — translates directly to a premium law firm visual identity.

The metaphor writes itself: Brasília was built from nothing into something extraordinary by visionary design. The very courts that Erico argues before sit in buildings designed by one of the 20th century's greatest architects. A law firm that knows what those buildings mean — and draws from that visual grammar — signals something different from every generic competitor in São Paulo or Rio.

07 — Strategy

Three positioning
directions

Before a single pixel is designed, Erico needs a positioning statement that can anchor the brand. These are the three most defensible directions — and they are not mutually exclusive.

A

"The arbitration practice built for Brazil's new economy."

Lean into Uber, iFood, tech companies. Arbitration as the smarter, faster, more confidential alternative for companies that cannot afford public court battles. This is the market no one else is serving explicitly — and it's the growth sector of Brazilian commercial law.

Best if: Erico wants to be known as the tech and startup arbitration specialist.

B

"Where arbitration meets the Superior Courts."

Unique to his geography. Only in Brasília can you have a single lawyer who handles private arbitration AND appeals before the STJ and STF. That dual access is rare — most arbitration specialists in São Paulo are not present when the award gets challenged in the superior courts.

Best if: Erico wants to differentiate on his Brasília-specific structural advantage.

C

"One lawyer. Full accountability."

Solo practice framed as a feature, not a limitation. No handoffs. No junior associates. The person you hire is the person who handles your case. Premium positioning through scarcity and direct access — a model impossible for large firms to replicate.

Best if: Erico wants to lead with the relationship and personal accountability model.
